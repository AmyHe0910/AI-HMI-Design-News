Ready for the world ft. Vicki Tan | Config 2026

The map was wrong. That was the first thing Vicki Tan realized. Not a literal map, but the one she had been given for how to change a mind. She had spent years collecting behavioral science’s sharpest tools: cognitive biases, those secret codes that explain why we procrastinate, cling to bad jobs, and sabotage our own happiness. She could recite loss aversion, status quo bias, the denomination effect—the whole cognitive bias codex, a sprawling taxonomy of mental glitches that looks like a periodic table of human irrationality. And yet, here she was, walking the streets of Fort Greene, Brooklyn, with a brilliant friend who desperately wanted to quit her full-time job and simply couldn’t. Tan had all the explanations. None of them worked.

There is a feeling Tan has been trying to name for a long time. “A kind of restlessness,” she says, “but that’s not quite the right word. It’s more directional. It isn’t anxiety or boredom—it’s something in you that wants to go somewhere, even if you can’t yet say where.” Jungian psychology offers a name for this: a floating charge of energy not attached to its right object. The idea, first described by Marie-Louise von Franz, Carl Jung’s closest collaborator, describes a force that ought to be directed toward something meaningful, an energy with a destination it hasn’t found yet. In traditional Chinese medicine, it’s called stagnant chi—creative energy in the body that can’t quite find its channel. Some people feel it as a flutter in the chest that won’t settle; others, as an emotional pull toward something just beyond reach. For Tan, it manifested as a specific obsession: the gap between knowing what you should do and actually doing it.

A product designer by trade, Tan had built her early career in user research, fascinated by the machinery of decision-making. Cognitive biases, to her, felt like keys. If you could recognize the moment your brain slipped into autopilot, you might be able to choose differently. Yet as a designer, she also knew a deeper truth: explaining a mechanism rarely changes behavior. Designers alter people’s actions every day, not by delivering lectures on human psychology, but by shaping the environment in which decisions unfold. The cognitive bias codex might be fascinating to stare at, but nobody consults it at the kitchen table when they’re agonizing over whether to leave their job. Tan decided to build something more tangible—something you could hold in your hands.

She started with a movable wheel. Spin it, and a single cognitive bias and its strategy would appear. That evolved into interactive spreads where pulling a tab or rotating a page would reveal a bias operating in everyday life, like lifting a curtain on your own mind. Soon it became a pocket guide, then a full manuscript, and eventually a published hardcover book called Ask This Book a Question. But that is the social-media version of the story. What actually happened in the years between concept and bound volume involved a global pandemic, thousands of design iterations, and a dinner that changed everything.

That night in Brooklyn, Tan made her friend mapo tofu, the Taiwanese dish her mother cooked when she was growing up. She talked about how, during a period of time off, she had been eating the same meal day after day. It wasn’t monotony—it was comfort. Returning to familiar flavors when so much else had been upended was its own kind of grounding. Then she described how she had structured her sabbatical around a self-reinforcing budget: instead of a rigid timeline, the more self-sustaining her projects became, the more freedom she earned to explore. Tofu and spreadsheets, side by side. And something shifted. At that table, quitting finally felt like something her friend could do. The enormous, paralyzing abstraction of a career change dissolved into the small, manageable arithmetic of daily choices.

Tan recognized the mechanism instantly. It was the denomination effect, a bias that causes us to overweight large, one-off decisions while undervaluing the small, recurring ones that accumulate into a life. The big career leap felt insurmountable; what was actually needed was a series of tiny, everyday steps. “Naming the bias wasn’t that important,” Tan now says. “But feeling it—her being able to recognize it in herself—that was what mattered.” The insight reoriented Tan’s entire project. Her book didn’t need to teach cognitive biases explicitly. It needed to help people experience the lived, applied wisdom of behavioral science, to see themselves in the stories and, through that recognition, find a way forward. Mapo tofu became the first story she wrote. The rest of the book grew around the same principle: lead with the personal and the possible, not the theoretical.

The result is a choose-your-own-adventure for grown-ups. Ask This Book a Question invites you to bring a stuck question—Why can’t I quit my job? How do I regain my creative energy?—and pass it through a series of lenses. You’re asked to consider the question’s domain, its urgency, the way it intersects with other parts of your life. Each path leads to a story from Tan’s own experience or from her mentors: the mapo tofu chapter, a story about her dog Charlie, and others. At the end of each tale, the biases that surfaced in real life are named, and if you’re curious, you can turn to a page that explains how that bias operates—what Tan calls its light and its shadow, the Jungian idea of making conscious what was unconscious.

But making the book meant doing something Tan had never done before: writing a book proposal. A trained writer might have started with the words, but words were the part she felt least equipped to handle. “Luckily,” she recalls, “writing is just one part of a book. There is helping the reader understand, connecting with what they care about, making difficult things easier to grasp—and that is all what design is.” So her proposal looked like a flowchart, navigable the way an app or a website is navigable. That structure became the seed of the choose-your-own-adventure format. It was unmistakably the work of a designer thinking on the page.

Still, she worried the book didn’t look enough like a book. She asked illustrator friends to help—one layered line work onto clay for a tactile feel, another drew playful depictions of Tan and her dog. The publisher’s response was consistent: the images didn’t match her voice. They felt too polished, too professional. “Isn’t that good?” Tan thought. The same thing happened with the cover. She brainstormed clever concepts, hired a professional designer, and kept hearing the same gentle verdict: the covers weren’t wrong, but they weren’t hers. The one design the publisher kept returning to was a blue door Tan had sketched early on—a drawing so unrefined she eventually regretted ever showing it. “By the end, I began to agree, begrudgingly, that the book had me inside and out,” she says. The finished volume went to print with her own unpolished illustrations and her non-literary prose. “It wasn’t perfect, but it was unmistakably mine.”

What made the book hers wasn’t the polish. It was that she knew the stories, she was the one who saw the biases in them, and she felt the connections between them. The visual language had to come from her, too, because that is how a story finds its listener. For anyone who has ever felt that their skills don’t quite match the thing they long to make, Tan’s experience is a quiet manifesto: “You can take what you already know, your skills, your way of thinking, even the things you’ve been shying away from, and pair them with that energy we’ve been talking about. You’ll start to see how capable you are once that energy finds its right object.”

Yet the hardest challenge wasn’t learning to draw, or even learning to write. It was what the writing asked her to face. As she mapped questions from early readers into topics and subtopics to fill the book’s non-linear structure, she noticed a pattern. Certain subjects kept sinking to the bottom of the pile: emotions, relationships, purpose in life. “On what authority was I going to write about these things that I had not yet figured out myself?” she remembers thinking. The avoidance wasn’t about technical difficulty. It was about the personal cost of going there.

In the final months, with her deadline looming, Tan opened an old journal and found a memory she had jotted down years before the book was even an idea. She was in elementary school, shopping for back-to-school supplies with her father. She adored the store’s precise organization, everything nestled in its designated drawer. That day, for reasons she couldn’t explain, she took ten sticks of Pentel 0.5 mm mechanical pencil lead and hid them inside another tube. At checkout, the clerk noticed. He looked directly at Tan and asked her father if he knew what had happened. Without missing a beat, her father came to her defense. Standing mute, Tan felt a simultaneous swelling of pride and cowering shame.

Her father died suddenly when she was almost twelve, on a Sunday morning while playing basketball. In the years that followed, the memory went quiet. When she first tried to write it down, it fell flat on the page—no ending, no energy. Returning to it with the discipline of the book, with a real reason to look harder, something changed. The memory came back differently: more vivid, textured, as if remade in the act of looking. A meditation teacher once reframed the word remembering for her—not as recalling, but as re-membering, reconnecting with the parts of yourself you had forgotten, the members you had cut off. To feel the sensations your body had blocked is to become whole again. Once Tan stopped treating those feelings as interruptions and started letting them enter, she began to recover other lost moments, “like a frog hopping in the rain, not thinking of the millions of small drops as terrible weather, but as the immense, unstoppable rush of life.”

The story of the Pentel lead became one of the last she wrote. It was the chapter on emotions, on feeling things again. Sharing it was frightening, but afterward, the rest of the writing opened. Not easier, exactly, but with more room to breathe. “Something shifted,” Tan says. “It wasn’t dramatic or life-changing, but I think what changed inside was my capacity to notice. I could finally sit with the feeling of being both terrified and excited and then do something with it. That’s where the energy goes—not into the thing, but really through you.”

This rhythm of pursuit and transformation follows a pattern Tan cherishes, one she used to structure her talk and, in a sense, her entire creative process. It’s called Kishōtenketsu, a four-part narrative form derived from classical Chinese poetry. Unlike the hero’s journey we are usually fed, Kishōtenketsu doesn’t end with a triumphant climax where everything is resolved and the hero rides into the sunset. Instead, it moves through introduction, development, a turn, and a conclusion that doesn’t tie things up so much as let them land. The change comes from inside, from what you notice, not from an external victory. You are altered, and then you begin again.

The clues to where your own floating charge of energy is trying to lead you are often easier to see in the people you love. Tan points to a friend, a design professor who started a tiny Taiwanese micro-farm with her mother, teaching neighbors how to cook with local plants and even making soap from cactus. Another friend, a design manager and former musician, can’t stop building small tools for artists—setlist trackers and stems libraries. These projects, born from the specific intersections of their lives, are things only they could have made. “Those are the types of things,” Tan says, “that only they in their particular lives could have made by following their energy.” Your version of this may already be forming, the idea that swam into your mind the moment she introduced the concept and has been percolating ever since. If you can’t quite see it yet, ask the people closest to you. Sometimes they see you more clearly than you see yourself.

When the floating charge finds its direction, the question stops following you, and you start following it. To carry that invitation beyond the final page, Tan often reads a poem by David Whyte, “Everything Is Waiting for You.” She offers it as a reminder that the smallest, most familiar objects are already in conversation with your potential:

Your great mistake is to act the drama
as if you were alone.
As if life were a progressive and cunning crime
with no witness to the tiny hidden transgressions.
To feel abandoned is to deny
the intimacy of your surroundings.
Surely, even you at times have felt the grand array,
the swelling presence and the chorus
crowding out your solo voice.
You must note the way the soap dish enables you,
or the window latch grants you freedom.
Alertness is the hidden discipline of familiarity.
The stairs are your mentor of things to come,
the doors have always been there
to frighten you and invite you,
and the tiny speaker in the phone
is your dream ladder to divinity.
Put down the weight of your aloneness and ease into
the conversation.
The kettle is singing even as it pours you a drink,
the cooking pots have left their arrogant aloofness and
seen the good in you at last.
All the birds and the creatures of the world are
unutterably themselves.
Everything, everything is waiting for you.

Tan’s book exists because she stopped waiting for the right credentials, the polished prose, the professional illustrations that would make her feel legitimate. She used what she already had—design thinking, a behavioral scientist’s curiosity, memories she was afraid to touch—and let the energy move through her. The result isn’t perfect, and it was never meant to be. It is unmistakably hers. And that, she suggests, is the only permission any of us needs.
