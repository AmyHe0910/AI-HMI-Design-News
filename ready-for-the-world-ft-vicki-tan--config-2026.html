The Floating Charge: Following the Energy That Leads You Home

There is a feeling that many of us carry but struggle to name. It is not exactly restlessness, nor anxiety, nor boredom. It is directional—a quiet, persistent pull toward something you cannot yet articulate. Jungian psychology calls this “a floating charge of energy not attached to its right object,” a phrase coined by Marie-Louise von Franz, Carl Jung’s closest collaborator. In Chinese traditional medicine, it is known as stagnant chi: creative energy in the body that has not found its channel. Some feel it as a flutter in the chest that refuses to settle. Others experience it as an emotional tug toward something just beyond reach, or a quiet obsession you cannot stop tinkering with. This talk—originally delivered by product designer and author Vicki Tan at Figma’s Config 2026—is the story of what happened when she decided to follow that energy, and how that choice led her to create something unmistakably hers.

有一种感受，许多人都在承载，却难以言明。那不完全是躁动，不是焦虑，也不是无聊。它带有方向性——一种安静而持续的牵引，将你拉向某个尚无法说清的地方。荣格心理学称其为“尚未附着于正确客体的一股游离能量”，这个说法来自卡尔·荣格最亲密的合作者玛丽-路易丝·冯·弗朗茨。在中国传统医学中，这被称为气滞：体内尚未找到通道的创造能量。有人感受到的是胸口不肯平息的悸动。有人体验到的是一种情感的牵引，指向某种刚好够不着的东西，又或者是一种让你不停摆弄的安静痴迷。本篇演讲——最初由产品设计师兼作家Vicki Tan在Figma的Config 2026大会上发表——正是关于当她决定跟随那股能量时发生了什么，以及这个选择如何引领她创造出独属于她自己的作品。

Tan had long been fascinated by questions of self-coherence. Why don’t we always act in our own best interest? What is really going on inside us when we get stuck? This curiosity had drawn her to study behavioral science and to begin her career in research. To her, cognitive biases felt like a set of secret codes—keys to noticing when your brain is on autopilot. But she also saw the problem: knowing something intellectually rarely changes our behavior. As designers, we change people’s behavior every day not by explaining how things work, but by showing how it is done. Tan wanted to make something tangible, something you could hold in your hands, that might actually help people change through the lens of cognitive bias. So began a journey that would stretch across years, a pandemic, and eventually into a published hardcover book titled Ask This Book a Question—a choose-your-own-adventure guide to life’s big and little decisions, designed entirely in Figma.

Tan长久以来一直对自我一致性这个问题着迷。为什么我们并不总是按照自己的最佳利益行事？当我们卡住时，内心究竟发生了什么？这份好奇心促使她学习行为科学，并在职业生涯初期从事研究工作。在她看来，认知偏见就像一组秘密代码——是察觉你大脑处于自动驾驶状态的关键。但她也看到了问题所在：理性上的了解极少能真正改变我们的行为。作为设计师，我们每天都在改变人们的行为，不是靠解释事物如何运作，而是通过展示如何去做。Tan想做出某种可触摸的东西，某种你可以拿在手里的东西，能够透过认知偏见的透镜真正帮助人们改变。于是，一场跨越数年、穿越疫情、最终变成一本精装书的旅程开始了，这本书叫《Ask This Book a Question》——一本关于人生大大小小决策的“自选冒险”指南，全书完全在Figma中设计完成。

The social-media version of this story is a clean arc: idea, concept, prototype, proposal, manuscript, book. The real story, however, is messier and far more instructive. Tan spent years stuck between the initial concept and a viable proposal. The breakthrough did not come from a design epiphany at her desk. It came over a home-cooked meal in Brooklyn. A friend wanted to quit her full-time job but couldn’t bring herself to do it. Tan, faithful to her behavioral science training, kept trying to help by identifying the cognitive biases at play—loss aversion, status quo bias. Her friend understood these concepts perfectly well. And yet, she remained stuck. That evening, Tan cooked mapo tofu, the Taiwanese comfort dish her mother used to make. Over dinner, she talked about how she had structured her time off around a self-reinforcing budget, where the more self-sustaining her projects became, the more freedom she had to explore. Mapo tofu was part of that math. Somewhere between the tofu and the spreadsheet, quitting finally felt possible to her friend. It was no longer a terrifying monolith but a small, navigable step.

这个故事在社交媒体上的版本是一条干净的弧线：想法、概念、原型、提案、手稿、成书。然而，真实的故事更加混乱，也更有教益。Tan在初始概念和可行提案之间卡了好几年。突破并不是在书桌前产生的设计顿悟，而是在布鲁克林的一顿家常饭中到来的。一位朋友想辞去全职工作，却无法迈出那一步。Tan忠于她的行为科学训练，不断尝试通过识别起作用的认知偏见来帮忙——损失厌恶、现状偏差。朋友完全理解这些概念，但她仍然卡住。那天晚上，Tan做了麻婆豆腐，那是她母亲过去常做的台湾家常菜。饭桌上她聊起自己如何围绕一个自我强化的预算来安排休假：项目越是能自给自足，她能探索的自由就越大，麻婆豆腐是这笔账的一部分。在豆腐与电子表格之间的某处，辞职突然变得好像可以做到了。那不再是个可怕的庞然大物，而是一个小型的、可导航的步骤。

That moment crystallized something crucial for Tan. The book did not need to teach cognitive bias on its face. It needed to help people experience the lived, applied wisdom of behavioral science in a way they could place themselves inside. The bias at work in her friend’s situation was the denomination effect—our tendency to overweight large, dramatic things and undervalue small, recurring ones. The big career decision felt enormous, but the small everyday choices were what actually added up. Naming the bias wasn’t important. Feeling it, recognizing it in yourself—that was what mattered. Mapo tofu became the first story she wrote, and the rest of the book grew outward from that insight: lead with the personal and the possible.

那一刻为Tan凝聚了某种至关重要的东西。这本书不需要在表面上教授认知偏见。它需要帮助人们以一种能把自己代入其中的方式，去体验行为科学那活生生的、被应用着的智慧。在她朋友处境中起作用的偏见是“面额效应”——我们倾向于高估那些庞大而戏剧化的事，却低估那些微小而重复的事。那个重大的职业决策感觉无比沉重，但真正起作用的却是那些日常的小选择。给偏见命名并不重要。感受到它，在你自己身上认出它——这才会真正起作用。麻婆豆腐成了她写下的第一个故事，整本书也从那个洞察中生长出来：从个人的、可能的事物出发。

The book itself is a quiet marvel of design thinking. You begin with a question you feel stuck on—anything from “Why can’t I quit my job?” to “How do I regain my creative energy?” The book then asks you to pass that question through a series of lenses: by topic, by subtopic, by timing (is this urgent, or once-in-a-lifetime?), and by how those dimensions intersect. You navigate through pages that help you see your question differently until you land on a story that resonates, drawn from Tan’s own life or from her mentors. At the end of each story, she names the biases that appeared in situ. If you wish, you can follow a thread to learn about how that bias looks when it is working for you—what Tan calls its light—and when it is working against you—its shadow. The structure is an invitation to make conscious what was unconscious, just as Jung described.

这本书本身是设计思维的一个安静奇迹。你从一个让你卡住的问题开始——从“为什么我辞不掉这份工作？”到“我如何重获创造力？”书随后请你将这个问题穿过一系列透镜：按主题、按子主题、按时机（这件事是紧急的，还是一生只有一次的？）以及这些维度如何交叉。你在页面间穿行，每一页都帮你看待问题的方式产生一点不同，直到你找到一个能产生共鸣的故事，这些故事来自Tan自己的人生，或来自她的导师。每个故事结尾，她会点出在其中出现的偏见。如果你愿意，可以沿着线索进一步了解，这种偏见在为你服务时是什么样——Tan称之为它的光明面——以及它在与你作对时是什么样——它的阴影面。这种结构是一种邀请，让无意识的东西变得有意识，正如荣格所描述的。

The process of turning an idea into a real book required Tan to write a proposal, something she had never done before. A writer might start with words, but that was the part she felt least comfortable with—she had never taken a writing class. So she began with what she knew. She treated the proposal like a UX flow, a flowchart you navigate the same way you would an app or a website. That is where the choose-your-own-adventure structure took shape. But a book is more than its architecture. It needed visuals, illustration, a cover. Tan asked illustrator friends to help, and they delivered beautiful, polished work. One layered line work onto clay for a tactile feel. Another created playful illustrations of Tan and her dog Charlie. The publisher’s feedback was consistent and confounding: these did not match her voice. They felt too professional. Tan recalls thinking, “Isn’t that good?” But the answer, it turned out, was no.

把一个想法变成一本真正的书，需要Tan写一份出版提案，这是她从未做过的事。一个作家也许会从文字开始，但那恰恰是她最不自在的部分——她从未上过写作课。于是她从自己懂得的事情开始。她把提案当作一个用户体验流程来对待，做成了一个流程图，就像你浏览一个应用或网站那样去导航。自选冒险的结构就是在那里成型的。但一本书不只有它的架构，它还需要视觉、插画、封面。Tan请插画师朋友帮忙，他们交出了精美而专业的作品。一位在线条上叠加了黏土材质，创造出一种可触摸的感觉。另一位创作了Tan和她的狗Charlie的俏皮插画。出版商的反馈始终如一又令人困惑：这些与她的声音不匹配，它们感觉太专业了。Tan记得自己当时想：“这不正是好事吗？”但答案原来是否定的。

The same pattern repeated with the cover design. Tan generated concepts she thought were cool. She tried her hand at executing a few herself, but she is not a graphic designer and was unhappy with the results. She hired a professional cover designer who turned her concepts into beautiful compositions. Again, the publisher said they did not match her voice. They kept circling back to one early concept: a blue door. By the end of the process, Tan had grown to resent that blue door. She had seen so many iterations of it. Yet she began to agree, begrudgingly, that the book now had her inside and out. In the end, they went with her cover. They went with her unrefined illustrations and her non-literary writing. It was not perfect, but it was unmistakably hers. What made the book hers was not the polish. It was that she knew the stories, she saw the biases in them, and she felt the connection between them. The visual voice had to come from her, too, because that is how the story finds its listener. She had everything she needed already.

封面设计也重复了同样的模式。Tan生成了一些她认为很酷的概念，也亲手尝试做了几个，但她不是平面设计师，对结果并不满意。她聘请了一位专业的封面设计师，将她的概念变成了美丽的构图。出版商再次说与她的声音不匹配，他们反复回到一个早期的概念：一扇蓝色的门。到了这个过程的末尾，Tan已经对这扇蓝色门心生怨恨，她看过了太多它的变体。但她开始勉强同意，这本书现在从里到外都是她了。最终，他们用了她的封面，用了她那未经修饰的插画，和她那并非文学作品的文字。它不完美，但它毫无疑问是属于她的。让这本书成为“她的”，不是精细的打磨，而是她知道那些故事，她在其中看见了偏见，并且感觉到了它们之间的连接。视觉的声音也必须来自她，因为只有这样故事才能找到它的听众。她需要的一切，她原本就已经拥有。

Then came the hardest part. It was not the writing itself; it was what the writing asked of her. While researching the book, Tan gathered questions from people about what they were stuck on. She mapped those questions into topics and subtopics, filling in the book in a non-linear way. But there is no project manager when you write a book—just you and your subconscious, which had been quietly pushing certain topics to the bottom of the pile. The topics she was avoiding were not technically hard; they were personally hard. Emotions. Relationships. Purpose in life. On what authority was she going to write about things she had not yet figured out herself? It was a new kind of insecurity. In the final months, with the deadline looming, she began digging deeper.

然后最困难的部分来了。不是写作本身，而是写作向她提出的要求。在为书做调研时，Tan收集了人们关于他们卡在何处的问题。她将这些问答映射为话题和子话题，用非线性的方式填满了整本书。但写书没有项目经理——只有你，和你的潜意识，而后者一直在悄悄把某些话题推到文件堆的最底层。她一直回避的那些话题，不是技术上困难，而是个人意义上的困难。情感。关系。人生目标。她凭什么去写这些连她自己都还没搞明白的事情？那是一种全新的不安。在最后的几个月里，随着截稿日逼近，她开始挖得更深。

Buried in an old journal, years before the book was even an idea, Tan had jotted down a memory from elementary school. She was with her father in an office-supply store, back-to-school shopping. She recalled relishing the store’s precise organization, the little things in their designated drawers. That day, she had secretly taken ten sticks of Pentel 0.5 mm lead—perhaps the smallest item in the entire store—and hidden them inside another tube. At checkout, the clerk noticed and asked her father if he knew how that had happened, looking at her the whole time. Without missing a beat, her father came to her defense. Standing there mute, she felt the simultaneous swelling of pride and cowering shame, both at once. Her father died suddenly when she was almost twelve—on a Sunday morning, playing basketball, without warning. In the months and years after, all her memories of him went quiet.

在一本旧日记里，早在书的构想出现几年前，Tan草草记下了一段小学时的回忆。她和父亲在一家办公用品店里，采购开学的文具。她记得自己格外享受店里那种精确的收纳，小物件各安其位。那天她偷偷拿了十根Pentel 0.5毫米的铅笔芯——也许那是整间店里最不值一提的东西——把它们藏在另一个管子里。结账时，店员注意到了，问她父亲知不知道这是怎么发生的，目光一直落在她身上。父亲没有丝毫犹豫，站出来维护了她。她站在那儿一言不发，同时感到骄傲的膨胀和畏缩的羞耻，两者交织在一起。父亲在她快12岁时突然去世——一个周日早晨，在打篮球时，没有任何预兆。此后经年累月，关于他的一切记忆都沉寂了。

When Tan first tried to write that memory, it fell flat on the page. It had no ending, no energy. It was only with the book, coming back to it with a reason to look harder, that the memory returned differently—more vivid, more textured, as if remade in the act of looking. A meditation teacher once reframed the word “remembering” for her: not as recalling, but as literal re-membering—reconnecting with the parts of yourself you had forgotten, the members of yourself you had cut off. To feel the sensations in your body that you had blocked off is to become whole again. When that memory came back, more recollections, details, and feelings from that period of her life came with it. Once she stopped treating those feelings as interruptions and began letting them enter, she started to recover what she had lost. She describes it as being like a frog hopping in the rain, not thinking of the millions of small drops as terrible weather, but as the immense, unstoppable rush of life. The Pentel lead story became one of the last she wrote for the book. It was the one about emotions and about feeling things again. It was a little bit scary to share, but once it was out, the rest came differently—not easier, exactly, but with more space to explore.

当Tan最初尝试写下那段记忆时，它在纸面上显得平淡无力，没有结局，也没有能量。只有在写这本书的过程中，带着一个要仔细回看的原因重新面对它时，那记忆才以不同的方式归来——更鲜活，更有质感，仿佛在注视中被重新塑造过。一位冥想老师曾为她重新定义了“忆起”这个词：不是回忆，而是字面意义上的“重新连接”——与你已经遗忘的那部分自己重新连接，与你切掉的那些成员的自己重新连接。去感受你曾经封锁的身体感受，就是重新变得完整。当那段记忆回来时，她生命中那个时期的更多回忆、细节和感受也一并归来。一旦她停止将这些感受视为干扰，开始允许它们进入，她便开始找回曾经丢失的东西。她形容那就像一只在雨中跳跃的青蛙，不将那万千水滴看作糟糕的天气，而是视为那巨大而不可阻挡的生命奔流。Pentel铅笔芯的故事成了她为这本书写下的最后几个故事之一。那是关于情感，关于重新去感受的故事。分享出来有一点吓人，但一旦它被释放出来，其他的就来得不一样了——不一定是更容易，而是有了更多探索的空间。

What shifted in Tan afterward was not dramatic or externally life-changing. What changed inside was her capacity to notice. She could finally sit with the feeling of being both terrified and excited—and then do something with it. That, she suggests, is where the floating charge of energy goes: not into the thing, but through you. This is how tending to creativity works. You follow your curiosity. You use what you already have. You change as the work asks of you. And then you begin again.

在那之后，Tan身上发生的变化，既不戏剧化，从外部看也称不上改变了人生。内在变化的，是她觉察的能力。她终于能够安坐于那种既恐惧又兴奋的感受之中——然后为它做点什么。她认为，那正是游离能量最终的归宿：不是注入那个东西，而是流经你自己。照料创造力就是这样运作的：跟随你的好奇心，使用你已经拥有的东西，随着作品对你的要求而改变，然后重新开始。

The narrative structure Tan uses to tell her own story is called Kishōtenketsu, a four-part form originating in classical Chinese poetry and later adopted across East Asia. Unlike the hero’s journey we are usually fed—with its rising action, climax, and resolution—Kishōtenketsu offers no point at which everything resolves and you are done. There is a turn, a shift in perspective that comes from within, not from any external event. You change because of what you notice. The clues to your own floating energy, Tan says, are everywhere. Sometimes it is easier to see them in your closest friends. She points to a design professor friend who started a tiny Taiwanese micro-farm with her mother, teaching locals to cook with native plants, even making soap from cactus. Another friend, a design manager and former musician, keeps building small tools for artists—setlist trackers, stems libraries. These are the kinds of things only they, in their particular lives, could have made by following their energy.

Tan用来讲述自己故事的叙事结构叫做“起承转合”，这是一种源于中国古典诗歌、后来被整个东亚地区采用的四段形式。不同于我们惯常听到的英雄旅程——有上升的剧情、高潮和结局——“起承转合”没有一个万事得以解决、你大功告成的时刻。这里有一个“转”，一个从内部发生的视角转换，而不是来自任何外部事件。你因你所觉察到的而改变。Tan说，属于你自己的那股游离能量的线索无处不在。有时它们在好友身上更容易看见。她举例一位设计教授朋友，她和母亲一起开办了一家小小的台湾微型农场，教当地人用本土植物烹饪，甚至用仙人掌做肥皂。另一位朋友，是一位设计经理也曾是音乐人，他一直在为艺术家制作各种小工具——歌单追踪器、分轨素材库。这些事情，只有他们，在各自特定的生命轨迹中，跟随自己的能量，才可能创造出来。

If you cannot quite see your own energy yet, Tan offers a gentle nudge: ask your closest friends. Sometimes they can see you more clearly than you can see yourself. And if that still feels elusive, try looking near it or around it, not directly at it. When the floating charge of energy finally finds its direction, the question stops following you, and you start following it.

如果你还看不太清自己的那股能量，Tan给出了一个温和的提示：去问问你最亲近的朋友。有时候他们比你自己更能看清你。如果那仍然难以捉摸，试着挨着它去看，或在它周围去看，而不是直勾勾地盯着它。当那股游离能量终于找到它的方向时，问题就不再追着你跑了，而是你开始追随着它。

To close, Tan offers a poem by David Whyte, “Everything Is Waiting for You,” as an invitation to notice where your energy is leading you. She reads it slowly, a sip of water first, asking the audience to simply listen, eyes open or closed:

在结尾，Tan引用了大卫·怀特的诗歌《一切都在等候你》，以此邀请你留意你的能量正在引领你去往何处。她先喝了一口水，缓缓朗读，邀请听众只是听，无论睁眼还是闭眼：

“Your great mistake is to act the drama as if you were alone. As if life were a progressive and cunning crime with no witness to the tiny hidden transgressions. To feel abandoned is to deny the intimacy of your surroundings. Surely, even you at times have felt the grand array, the swelling presence and the chorus crowding out your solo voice. You must note the way the soap dish enables you or the window latch grants you freedom. Alertness is the hidden discipline of familiarity. The stairs are your mentor of things to come and the doors have always been there to frighten you and invite you. And the tiny speaker in the phone is your dream ladder to divinity. Put down the weight of your aloneness and ease into the conversation. The kettle is singing even as it pours you a drink.
