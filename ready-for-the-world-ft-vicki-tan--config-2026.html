The Floating Charge: Vicki Tan on Following the Energy That Has Nowhere to Go but Through You


漂浮的电荷：Vicki Tan 谈追随那种无处可去只能穿过你的能量


There is a feeling many of us know but struggle to name—a kind of restlessness that isn’t quite anxiety, boredom, or ambition. It’s more directional, a pull toward something you can’t yet articulate. The Jungian psychologist Marie-Louise von Franz, Carl Jung’s closest collaborator, called it a “floating charge of energy not attached to its right object.” In Chinese traditional medicine, it’s known as stagnant qi: creative energy in the body that can’t quite find its channel. For Vicki Tan, a product designer who stumbled into authorship, that floating charge led her to create something entirely unexpected—an illustrated, choose-your-own-adventure book about decision-making for adults, designed entirely in Figma.


有一种感受我们很多人都熟悉却又难以命名——一种既不是焦虑、无聊，也不是野心的躁动。它更具方向性，是一种朝向你还无法清晰表达的事物的引力。荣格心理学中，卡尔·荣格最亲密的合作者玛丽-路易丝·冯·弗朗兹将其称为“尚未附着于正确对象的漂浮电荷”。在中医里，它被称为气滞：身体中无法找到通道的创造能量。对于意外成为作者的产品设计师 Vicki Tan 来说，这股漂浮的电荷引领她创造出一种完全意想不到的东西——一本为成年人设计的、关于决策的插图版“选择你自己的冒险”书，而整本书完全是在 Figma 中设计的。


Speaking on the mezzanine stage at Figma’s Config 2026, Tan didn’t offer a tidy, step-by-step guide to making a book. Instead, she traced the messy, non-linear journey of turning a movable concept into print, a process that spanned years, a pandemic, and thousands of design iterations. But her talk was about more than publishing. It was an invitation to notice where your own energy is leading you—and to trust that you already have what you need to follow it. “There’s a version of this for you,” she told the audience. “You can take what you already know, your skills, your way of thinking, even the things you’ve been shying away from, and then you can pair them with that energy we’ve been talking about, and you’ll start to see how capable you are once that energy finds its right object.”


在 Figma 的 Config 2026 大会上，Tan 在夹层舞台上并没有提供一份制作书籍的整齐步骤指南。相反，她追溯了将一个可移动的概念转化为印刷品的混乱而非线性的旅程，这一过程跨越了数年、一场疫情，以及数千次设计迭代。但她的演讲远不止于出版。这是一份邀请，让你注意自己的能量正将你引向何方——并相信你已经拥有追随它所需的一切。“这对你来说也有一个版本，”她告诉观众，“你可以运用你已经知道的东西，你的技能，你的思维方式，甚至那些你一直回避的事情，然后把它们与我们一直在谈论的那种能量结合起来。一旦那股能量找到了它正确的对象，你就会开始看到自己有多大的能力。”


Tan’s own floating charge had long pulled her toward questions of self-coherence: Why don’t we always act in our own best interest? What’s going on inside us when we get stuck? It’s why she studied behavioral science and began her career in research. To her, cognitive biases felt like a set of secret codes—keys to noticing when your brain is on autopilot. Yet she also knew that simply naming a mechanism rarely changes behavior. “As designers, we change people’s behavior every day,” she said. “Not by explaining how things work, but by showing how it’s done.” She wanted to make something tangible, something you could hold in your hands, that might actually help people change through the lens of cognitive bias.


Tan 自己的漂浮电荷长期以来一直将她引向关于自我一致性的问题：为什么我们并不总是按照自己的最佳利益行事？当我们陷入困境时，内心到底在发生什么？这正是她学习行为科学并在职业生涯初期从事研究工作的原因。对她来说，认知偏见感觉就像一套秘密代码——是觉察你的大脑何时处于自动驾驶状态的关键。但她也知道，仅仅给一个机制命名很少能改变行为。“作为设计师，我们每天都在改变人们的行为，”她说，“不是通过解释事物如何运作，而是通过展示它是如何做到的。”她想要做出一些有形的、可以握在手中的东西，能够真正通过认知偏见的视角帮助人们改变。


The idea began as a movable cognitive bias wheel, where you could rotate a disc to explore each bias strategy one at a time. It evolved into interactive spreads with pull-tabs that revealed biases hiding in everyday situations, “kind of like pulling a curtain up on reality.” That turned into a pocket guide, which somehow turned into a full manuscript filled with words she had written. Eventually, it became a published hardcover book titled Ask This Book a Question. But that’s the social-media version of the story. In truth, Tan got stuck between concept and proposal for years, including a pandemic-induced break.


这个想法最初是一个可旋转的认知偏见轮盘，你可以通过转动圆盘一次探索一种偏见策略。它后来演变为带有拉环的互动式对页，揭示隐藏在日常生活情境中的偏见，“有点像拉开现实上的帷幕”。这又变成了一本袖珍指南，不知怎的又变成了一部满是文字的完整手稿，那些文字是她自己写出来的。最终，它成了一本正式出版的硬壳书，书名是《向这本书提问》。但这是社交媒体版本的故事。事实上，Tan 在概念和选题方案之间卡了好几年，其中包括一次因疫情导致的中断。


During that time, living in Brooklyn, Tan would walk through Fort Greene with a friend who desperately wanted to quit her full-time job. Playing the amateur behavioral scientist, Tan tried to help by identifying what might be keeping her stuck. “My rational brain kept thinking, if you could just explain loss aversion to her, if you could help her understand status quo bias, surely she would leave her job,” Tan recalled. But her friend understood cognitively what was happening—and yet she was stuck. One night, Tan made her friend mapo tofu, the Taiwanese dish her mother used to cook. Over dinner, she talked about how she’d structured her own time off around a self-reinforcing budget, and how eating the same comfort food every day wasn’t a chore but a return to familiar flavors when so much else had changed.


在那段时间里，住在布鲁克林的 Tan 会和朋友在格林堡散步，这位朋友非常想辞去全职工作。Tan 尝试用行为科学的方法帮助她，找出是什么让她卡住。“我理性的大脑一直在想，如果你能向她解释损失厌恶，如果你能帮她理解现状偏见，当然她就会辞职。”Tan 回忆道。但她的朋友在认知上明白正在发生什么——然而她还是卡住了。一天晚上，Tan 为朋友做了麻婆豆腐，这是她母亲以前常做的台湾菜。晚饭时，她谈到了自己如何围绕一个自我强化的预算来安排休假时间，以及每天吃同样的舒适食物并不是一件苦差事，而是在许多其他事情都改变了的时候，回归熟悉的味道。


Then something shifted. The friend finally felt like quitting was something she could do. The big, scary idea was no longer just big and scary. “Seeing it click for her, I think something really shifted for me, too,” Tan said. She realized the book didn’t need to teach cognitive bias on its face. It just needed to help people experience the lived, applied wisdom of behavioral science in a way they could place themselves in. The bias at work was the denomination effect—our tendency to overweight large things and undervalue small, recurring ones. The big career decision felt enormous, but the small, everyday choice was what added up. Naming the bias wasn’t important; feeling it, recognizing it in yourself, that was what mattered. Mapo tofu became the first story she wrote.


然后，某些东西发生了转变。这位朋友终于觉得辞职是她能够做到的事情。那个巨大而可怕的想法，不再仅仅是巨大而可怕的。“看到它在她身上奏效了，我觉得我自己也有些东西真正转变了。”Tan 说。她意识到这本书并不需要在表面上教人认知偏见。它只需要帮助人们以一种能够将自己代入的方式，去体验行为科学中那些活生生的、应用性的智慧。当时发挥作用的偏见是面额效应——我们倾向于高估大的事情，而低估小的、重复发生的事情。重大的职业决策感觉无比沉重，但细小的日常选择才是真正累积起来的。给偏见命名并不重要；感受到它，在自己身上认出它，才是关键。麻婆豆腐成了她写下的第一个故事。


The rest of the book grew around that principle: lead with the personal and the possible. Ask This Book a Question is a choose-your-own-adventure guide to life’s big and little decisions. You start with the question you’ve been stuck on—anything from “Why can’t I quit my job?” to “How do I regain my creative energy?” The book then asks you to pass your question through a series of lenses: by topic, by timing, by how those things intersect. Each path leads you to a story from Tan’s life or from her mentors. At the end of each story, the biases that appeared are named in situ. If you’re inclined, you can follow a thread to learn how that bias looks in its “light” (when it’s working for you) and its “shadow” (when it’s working against you). It’s a structure that echoes Jung’s idea of making conscious what was unconscious.


书的其余部分围绕这一原则展开：以个人的和可能的事物为引导。《向这本书提问》是一本关于人生大小决策的“选择你自己的冒险”指南。你从那个一直卡住你的问题开始——从“为什么我不能辞职？”到“我怎样才能重获创造力？”任何问题都可以。然后，这本书要求你将问题通过一系列镜头审视：按主题、按时间、按这些事物如何交汇。每条路径都会带你进入一个来自 Tan 自己或她导师生活中的故事。在每个故事结尾，现场出现的偏见会被点名。如果你有兴趣，可以沿着线索去了解这个偏见在“光明面”（当它对你有帮助时）和“阴影面”（当它对你不利时）是如何表现的。这种结构呼应了荣格关于让无意识成为意识的思想。


To turn an idea into a real book, the first step is a proposal, and Tan had never written one. A writer might start with words, but Tan was least comfortable with that. “Luckily, writing is just one part of the book,” she said. “There is helping the reader understand, there’s connecting with what they care about, there’s making difficult things easier to understand—and that is all what design is.” So she started with what she had: a proposal that looked like a flowchart, navigated like an app or a website. That’s where the choose-your-own-adventure structure took shape. The proposal did, she admitted, look a bit like UI at that point. But she knew she could make it better with visuals, illustration, and a cover.


将一个想法变成一本真正的书，第一步是写一份选题方案，而 Tan 从没写过。作家可能会从文字开始，但 Tan 对此最不自在。“幸运的是，写作只是书的一部分，”她说，“有帮助读者理解，有与他们关心的事物建立连接，有让困难的事物更容易理解——而这一切就是设计的全部。”于是她从她所拥有的开始：一份看起来像流程图的选题方案，像应用或网站一样导航。那就是“选择你自己的冒险”结构成形的地方。她承认，那个方案在那个时候确实有点像用户界面。但她知道可以通过视觉、插图和封面使它变得更好。


Deep in the writing, she asked illustrator friends for help. Ryan layered line work onto clay for a tactile feel. Karen drew playful illustrations of Tan and her dog, Charlie. Tan loved both styles, but the publisher said they didn’t match her voice—they felt too polished, too professional. “I remember thinking to myself, ‘Isn’t that good?’” Tan said. But it wasn’t. The cover process went the same way. She came up with concepts, tried her own hand, then hired a professional designer who made beautiful covers from her ideas. Again, the feedback was the same: they didn’t feel like her voice. The publisher kept returning to a blue door concept Tan had shown early on. “At this point in the process, I regretted ever showing the blue door,” she said. “I had seen so many iterations of this blue door.” Eventually, begrudgingly, she agreed that the book had to have her inside and out. They went with her cover, her unrefined illustrations, and her non-literary writing. It wasn’t perfect, but it was unmistakably hers.


在写作深入时，她请插画师朋友帮忙。Ryan 将线条叠加在粘土上，营造出一种触感。Karen 画了 Tan 和她的狗 Charlie 的好玩插图。Tan 喜欢这两种风格，但出版社说它们与她的声音不匹配——它们感觉太精致了，太专业了。“我记得我当时心想，‘这难道不是好事吗？’”Tan 说。但事实并非如此。封面的过程也一样。她提出了概念，自己尝试了一些，然后聘请了一位专业封面设计师，后者根据她的概念制作了漂亮的封面。同样，反馈还是一样：它们感觉不像她的声音。出版社不断回到 Tan 早期展示的一个蓝门概念。“在那个时候，我后悔曾经展示过那个蓝门，”她说，“我已经看过这个蓝门的无数次迭代了。”最终，她勉强同意，这本书必须由内而外地拥有她。他们用了她的封面，她那未加修饰的插图，以及她那非文学性的文字。它不完美，但毫无疑问是属于她的。


“What made the book mine wasn’t the polish,” Tan said. “It was that I knew the stories. And I was the one who saw the biases in them, and I felt the connection between them.” The visual had to come from her, because that’s how the story finds the listener. In the end, it was just her using what she already had.


“让这本书属于我的，不是精致度，”Tan 说，“而是我了解那些故事。我是那个在其中看到偏见的人，我感受到了它们之间的联系。”视觉必须来自她，因为故事就是这样找到听众的。最终，就是她在运用自己已经拥有的东西。


But the hardest part of writing the book, Tan discovered, wasn’t the writing itself. It was what the writing asked of her. When she researched the book, she gathered questions from people about what they were stuck on, mapped them into topics and subtopics, and used that to fill the book in a non-linear way. There is no project manager when you write a book, she noted. It’s just you and your subconscious—pushing certain topics to the bottom of the pile. The ones she avoided weren’t technically hard; they were personally hard. They were about emotions, relationships, purpose. “On what authority was I going to write about these things that I had not yet figured out myself?” she asked. It felt like a whole new type of insecurity.


但 Tan 发现，写这本书最困难的部分，并不是写作本身。而是写作对她提出的要求。当她为这本书做调研时，她从人们那里收集了他们卡住的问题，将它们映射到主题和副主题中，然后用这些以非线性的方式填充了这本书。她指出，当你写一本书时，没有项目经理。只有你和你的潜意识——把某些主题推到待办清单的最底层。她回避的那些主题并不是技术上难，而是个人层面上的难。它们关乎情感、关系、人生目的。“我有什么资格去写这些我自己都还没弄明白的事？”她问道。那感觉像是一种全新的不安全感。


In the very last months, with the deadline looming, she started digging deeper. Buried in an old journal, years before the book was even an idea, she found a memory of her father. She was in elementary school, shopping with him for back-to-school supplies. She remembered relishing the precise organization of the office goods store, the little things in their designated drawers. That day, she had sneakily taken ten sticks of Pentel 0.5 mm lead—perhaps the smallest item in the store—and hidden them inside another tube. She didn’t know why. When they checked out, the clerk noticed and asked her father if he knew how that had happened, looking at her the whole time. Without missing a beat, her father came to her defense. Standing there mute, she felt the simultaneous swelling of pride and cowering shame.


在最后几个月，随着截稿日期临近，她开始更深入地挖掘。在一本旧日记里，在书还只是一个想法之前的许多年，她埋藏着一段关于父亲的记忆。那时她上小学，和父亲一起去买开学用品。她记得自己非常享受那个办公用品商店里精确的组织方式，那些小物件放在它们指定的抽屉里。那天，她偷偷拿了十根 0.5 毫米的 Pentel 铅芯——也许是店里最小的东西——并把它们藏在另一个管子里。她不知道为什么。当他们结账时，店员发现了，问她的父亲知不知道这是怎么回事，同时一直看着她。她的父亲毫不犹豫地为她辩护。她站在那儿说不出话，感到骄傲和畏缩的羞耻同时涌上心头。


Tan’s father died suddenly when she was almost twelve, on a Sunday morning while playing basketball. In the years after, all her memories of him went quiet. She had tried to write down the office-supply-store memory once before, but it fell flat on the page—no ending, no energy. Only when she returned to it with the book, with a reason to look harder, did the memory come back differently. It was more vivid, textured, “as if it was remade in the act of looking.”


Tan 的父亲在她快十二岁时突然去世，那是一个星期天早晨，他打篮球时发生了意外。之后的年月里，她所有关于他的记忆都沉寂了。她曾尝试把那间办公用品店的记忆写下来，但它在纸上显得平淡无力——没有结局，没有能量。只有当她为了这本书，带着更努力去看的理由重新回到它时，那记忆才以不同的方式回来了。它更加生动，更有质感，“仿佛是在观看的行为中被重新塑造的”。


A meditation teacher once reframed the word “remembering” for her—not as recalling, but as literal re-membering, reconnecting with the parts of yourself you had forgotten or cut off. To feel the sensations in your body that you had blocked off is to become whole again. When that memory returned, more details and feelings from that part of her life came with it. “Once I stopped treating those feelings as interruptions and started letting them enter, I began to recover the memories that I had lost,” she said, comparing it to a frog hopping in the rain, not thinking of the millions of drops as terrible weather but as the immense, unstoppable rush of life. The Pentel lead story became one of the last she wrote. It was the one about emotions, about feeling things again. It was a little scary to share, but once it was out, the rest came with more space to explore.


一位冥想老师曾为她重新诠释了“记住”这个词——不是回忆，而是字面意义上的“重新-连接”（re-membering），重新与那些你已遗忘或切断的自我部分连接。去感受那些你曾经封闭的身体感觉，就是重新变得完整。当那段记忆回来时，她生命中那个时期的更多细节和感受也随之而来。“一旦我不再把那些感受当作干扰，而是开始让它们进来，我就开始找回我丢失的记忆了。”她说，将之比作一只在雨中跳跃的青蛙，不是把那无数的雨滴想成糟糕的天气，而是视为宏大、不可阻挡的生命奔流。Pentel 铅芯的故事成了她最后写下的故事之一。那是关于情感的故事，关于重新去感受。分享它有点吓人，但一旦它被说出来，其余的故事就有了更多探索的空间。


Something shifted for Tan after that. It wasn’t dramatic or life-changing, but her capacity to notice grew. She could sit with the feeling of being both terrified and excited and then do something with it. “That’s where the energy goes,” she said. “Not into the thing, but really through you.” This, she realized, is how tending to creativity works: you follow your curiosity, you use what you already have, you change as the work asks of you, and then you begin again.


在那之后，Tan 的内心发生了变化。这变化并不戏剧化，也谈不上改变人生，但她觉察的能力增长了。她能与既害怕又兴奋的感觉共处，然后用它做点什么。“能量就是去往那里的，”她说，“不是进入那个东西，而是真正地穿过你。”她意识到，这正是呵护创造力的方式：追随你的好奇心，运用你已经拥有的东西，随着工作对你的要求而改变，然后重新开始。


To structure her talk, Tan drew on kishōtenketsu, a four-part narrative form from classical Chinese poetry. Unlike the Western hero’s journey, there is no point at which everything resolves and you’re done. You change because of what you notice, because of a turn that comes from the inside, not from anything outside you. The clues to your energy are everywhere, she said. Sometimes they’re easier to see in your closest friends. One of her friends, a design professor, started a tiny Taiwanese micro-farm with her mother, teaching locals to cook with native plants and even making soap from cactus. Another, a design manager and former musician, keeps making little tools for artists: setlist trackers, stems libraries. “These are the types of things that only they, in their particular lives, could have made by following their energy.”


为了构建她的演讲，Tan 借鉴了“起承转合”，这是一种源自中国古典诗歌的四段式叙事形式。与西方的英雄之旅不同，它没有一切得到解决、万事大吉的时刻。你的改变源于你所觉察到的，源于一种来自内在的转折，而非任何外在的东西。她说，你自身能量的线索无处不在。有时它们在你最亲密的朋友身上更容易看到。她的一个朋友是设计教授，和母亲一起开办了一个小小的台湾微型农场，教当地人用本土植物烹饪，甚至用仙人掌做肥皂。另一个朋友是设计经理，曾是音乐人，他一直在为艺术家制作小工具：演出曲目追踪器、分轨素材库。“这些事情，只有他们，在他们各自独特的生活中，追随自己的能量才能做出来。”


Tan shared these examples to underscore that you have your own floating charge, too. It’s the thing that came to mind when she introduced the idea. If you can’t quite see your own yet, ask your closest friends—sometimes they can see you more clearly than you can see yourself. When that floating charge finds its direction, the question stops following you, and you start following it.


Tan 分享这些例子，是为了强调你也有你自己的漂浮电荷。它就是当她介绍这个概念时你脑海中浮现的那个东西。如果你还看不太清楚自己的，那就问最亲密的朋友——有时他们能比你自己更清楚地看见你。当那股漂浮电荷找到了它的方向，问题就不再追着你跑，而是你开始追随它。


Tan closed her talk not with a call to action but with an invitation to feel.
