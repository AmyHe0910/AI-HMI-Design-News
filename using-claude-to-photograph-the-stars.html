Using Claude To Photograph The Stars

Chasing the Ghost Light: A Photographer, an AI Companion, and the Milky Way That Remained Unseen


It begins with a gamble. Under the immense, ink-black dome of Death Valley—a place so vast and quiet that a single misstep can mean getting lost for hours—Shane Auckland is staring at a sky that looks, to the naked eye, like an ordinary scatter of stars. The Milky Way, that great luminous river of our galaxy, is actually invisible to human eyes. It’s a ghost. Yet he is here to photograph it, to bend the night’s feeble light into a sweeping panorama that arches over the desert mountains. His co-pilot on this quest isn’t a veteran astrophotographer whispering advice in the darkness; it’s an AI named Claude, which gave him a set of bullet points on how to achieve the impossible.


这始于一场豪赌。在死亡谷广袤无垠、漆黑如墨的穹顶之下——这个地方如此辽阔寂静，稍不留神就可能在荒野中迷路数小时——肖恩·奥克兰正凝视着一片对肉眼来说只是寻常星空的夜幕。银河，那条属于我们星系的壮丽光河，实际上是人眼无法直接看见的。它就像一个幽灵。然而，他来这里正是为了拍摄它，用镜头捕捉那微弱的夜光，编织成一幅拱悬在沙漠群山之上的全景图。他此次任务的副手，并非在黑暗中低语指导的资深星空摄影师，而是一个名叫Claude的人工智能，它曾为肖恩列出了一份如何实现这一切的要点清单。


“My hobbies are usually pretty intense. I like things that are hard, that I have to practice a lot,” Auckland says. There’s no pretense of casual dabbling. Astrophotography, for him, is a deliberate act of seeking discomfort—driving into the middle of nowhere, setting up fragile gear in the cold, and surrendering to conditions you cannot control. “It’s the roll of the dice and you go with the wind. Everything really has to align, like the stars have to align.” The pun is fully intended, but the truth is dead serious: a passing cloud, a battery that dies in the chill, a miscalculated exposure—any tiny variable can vaporize a night’s work.


“我的爱好通常都非常硬核。我喜欢那些困难重重、需要大量练习才能掌握的事情。”奥克兰说。他没有假装这只是随便玩玩。对他来说，星空摄影是一场刻意寻求不适感的修行——开车深入无人之境，在寒冷中架设脆弱的设备，然后向那些你无法控制的自然条件低头。“这就像掷骰子，然后随风而动。所有的一切都必须完美配合，就像星体也要连成一线。”他故意用了双关，但其中的道理却是极其严肃的：一片飘过的云、一块在低温中罢工的电池、一次计算失误的曝光——任何微小的变量都能让一整晚的辛劳化为泡影。


Before setting out, Auckland turned to artificial intelligence. Claude, the AI assistant, provided the kind of distilled knowledge that once required years of mentorship or fathoms-deep dives into arcane photography forums. It explained the core principles: how to set the exposure long enough to drink in photons from the galactic core, yet short enough to avoid star trails as the Earth rotates. It laid out the geometry of a panorama—how to rotate the camera by about 30 degrees between shots so that each frame overlaps enough to be stitched together seamlessly later. In a few bullet points, Auckland got a roadmap. The AI didn’t replace creativity or courage, but it collapsed the steep learning curve that keeps so many curious people from ever buying a tripod and heading into the night.


在出发之前，奥克兰求助于人工智能。Claude这位AI助手，为他提供了那种曾经需要数年师徒相授，或者深潜进晦涩的摄影论坛才能掌握的浓缩知识。它解释了核心原理：如何将曝光时间设置得足够长，以吸收来自星系核心的光子，同时又要足够短，以避免因地球自转而产生的星轨。它展示了全景图的几何构图法——如何在每次拍摄时将相机旋转大约30度，使得每张照片之间有足够的重叠，以便后期能无缝拼接起来。仅凭几条要点清单，奥克兰就获得了一张路线图。AI并没有取代创造力或勇气，但它铲平了那道陡峭的学习曲线，而正是这道曲线，曾让无数充满好奇的人始终没能买下三脚架、奔赴夜色。


Death Valley National Park is not a forgiving stage. It is a landscape of extremes, where the day’s scorching heat gives way to a nocturnal chill, and where the darkness is so profound it becomes a physical presence. Auckland set up his shot anticipating where the Milky Way would emerge, aiming to frame an entire mountain ridge beneath the galaxy’s eventual arch. The quiet was absolute. “Usually when you’re doing this, you’re in the middle of nowhere and it is super quiet. And that is like such an awesome feeling,” he recalls. In that lonesome silence, the act of photography transforms into something almost meditative—a solitary vigil against the immensity of space.


死亡谷国家公园不是一个温和的舞台。它是一片极端的土地，白天的酷热在入夜后让位于刺骨的寒冷，而那里的黑暗是如此深邃，几乎成了一种有形的实体。奥克兰预判着银河将会升起的位置来架设机位，试图将整条山脊置于星系拱桥的笼罩之下。四周静得可怕。“通常当你做这件事的时候，你身处荒郊野外，超级安静。那真是一种太棒的感觉，”他回忆道。在那孤寂的静默中，摄影的行为演变成了一种近乎冥想的仪式——一场对抗宇宙无垠空间的孤独守夜。


Then comes the moment of revelation. With the naked eye, Auckland could see only a misty suggestion of the stars. But the camera’s lens, with its aperture opened wide like an unblinking mechanical pupil, devours the night differently. “With the naked eye you actually really can’t see the Milky Way, but with the lens you can open that aperture all the way open and allow all that light to come in, and all of a sudden you’re seeing outer space.” The distinction is staggering. Our biological eyes are feeble instruments for the cosmos; the camera, guided by a human who has learned the rules from an AI, becomes a time machine that collects light across 25 seconds—or longer—and conjures a universe that was always there, just beyond the threshold of human perception.


接着，启示的时刻来临了。用肉眼望去，奥克兰只能看到星辰的朦胧暗示。但是相机的镜头，将其光圈像一只永不眨眼的机械瞳孔那样完全张开，以一种截然不同的方式吞噬着黑夜。“用肉眼，你真的几乎看不见银河，但是通过镜头，你可以把光圈开到最大，让所有的光线涌进来，突然间，你就能看见外太空了。”这种差异令人震惊。我们的生物眼睛在宇宙面前是一件孱弱的仪器；而相机，在那位从AI那里学得了规则的摄影师指引下，变成了一台时光机器，在25秒——甚至更长的时间里收集着光线，召唤出了一个一直存在、却始终徘徊在人类感知门槛之外的宇宙。


The process is stubborn and iterative. “I’m going to rotate about 30 degrees, I think should be enough to overlap the panorama photo,” he narrates in the dark. “25 second exposure. We’re definitely not there yet. A little longer I think.” There’s no instant gratification. Each test shot is a conversation with the camera’s LCD screen, a negotiation between what the engineer planned and what the sky will give. The AI provided the blueprint, but the photographer must improvise the final dance—tweaking the exposure by gut feel, guessing at the sky’s true brightness, trusting that the overlapping frames will later stitch into a perfect window spanning the heavens.


这个过程是固执且充满迭代的。“我要转大约30度，我觉得应该足够重叠全景照片需要的画面了，”他在黑暗中讲述着。“25秒曝光。肯定还没到位。我觉得还得再长一点点。”这里没有即时满足。每一次试拍都是一场与相机液晶屏幕的对话，是工程师的计算与天空的恩赐之间的一场协商。AI提供了蓝图，但摄影师必须即兴完成最终的舞蹈——凭直觉微调曝光，猜测天空真正的亮度，并且相信那些重叠的画面最终会拼接成一扇横跨天宇的完美窗口。


Failure is not just a possibility; it is a necessary condition. Auckland’s mantra is blunt: “You’re gonna fail, but you got to keep going and keep pushing forward and just keep thinking.” This isn’t empty motivation. It’s the realistic engineering of hope. In a pursuit where the celestial target is ghostly, the environment hostile, and the technique borrowed from a machine collaborator, failure is simply data—a sharper read on what the next exposure must be. The stars do not care if you get the shot. That indifference is the very reason the chase is meaningful.


失败不仅是一种可能性，更是一种必要条件。奥克兰的座右铭很直白：“你肯定会失败的，但你必须继续前进，继续努力，并且不停地思考。”这并非空洞的励志鸡汤。这是对希望的务实构建。在一个天体目标如幽灵般缥缈、环境恶劣、技术又是从机器伙伴那里借来的追求中，失败不过是一种数据——是对下一次曝光必须如何调整的更清晰指引。众星并不在乎你是否能拍下它们。而这种冷漠，恰恰让这场追逐变得意义非凡。


Back at a computer, the work will continue. The shots that are eventually captured—assuming the dice roll in his favor—will need to be fed into software to be stitched into that seamless panorama, the digital tapestry where photons from the galactic center are woven into a stable arch. The AI helped him begin his quest; another set of algorithms will help him finish it. We’re living in an era where the most ancient human impulse—to look up at the night sky and seek its secrets—is being amplified by intelligences both human and artificial. Shane Auckland, standing alone in one of the darkest places on Earth, hoping the next frame is the one, represents the new romantic adventurer: someone who uses the latest digital oracles not to escape reality, but to see more of it. “I hope we can get it tonight,” he says, the “we” perhaps referring to himself and his gear, or perhaps to the curious partnership of man and machine. “That would be so sick. I’d be very excited.”


回到电脑前，工作仍将继续。假设骰子最终掷出了好运，那些被捕捉到的照片将被导入软件，拼接成一幅无缝的全景图——一幅由银河中心的光子编织成稳定拱门的数字织锦。AI帮他开启了这场探索；另一组算法将帮助他收尾。我们正生活在一个这样的时代：人类最古老的冲动——仰望夜空、探寻其秘密——正在被人类与人工智能的双重智慧所放大。肖恩·奥克兰独自站在地球上最黑暗的角落之一，期盼着下一张照片就是那张完美的作品，他代表了新一代的浪漫冒险家：他们使用最新的数字神谕，不是为了逃避现实，而是为了看见更多的现实。“我希望今晚我们能拍到，”他说，句中的“我们”或许是指他和他的器材，或许是指人与机器这对奇妙的搭档。“那可就太牛了。我会非常兴奋的。”
