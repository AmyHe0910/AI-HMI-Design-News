Using Claude To Photograph The Stars

Chasing the Ghost Light: A Beginner, an AI, and the Milky Way Over Death Valley

Shane Auckland describes his hobbies with a kind of cheerful masochism: “usually pretty intense. I like things that are hard, that I have to practice a lot.” It’s a disposition that suits his latest obsession—astrophotography—almost too well. The craft involves driving deep into wilderness where the sky still means something, setting up fragile equipment in total darkness, and waiting. You are gambling, he says: “It’s the roll of the dice and you go with the wind. Everything really has to align—like the stars have to align.”

But there’s a ghost in every frame. The Milky Way, that great luminous backbone of our galaxy, is essentially invisible to the naked eye. You can stand in the most lightless desert on Earth, look up, and see only a scatter of brighter stars and perhaps a faint, milky smudge if you know exactly where to search. To reveal the river of starlight—the dust lanes, the nebulae, the core blazing like a distant metropolis—you have to let your camera see for you. And to learn how to do that, Auckland turned not to a seasoned mentor, but to an artificial intelligence named Claude.

Auckland’s goal was absurdly ambitious for a newcomer. He didn’t just want a single photograph of the night sky. He wanted to create a panorama: a sweeping, high-resolution composite that would capture the Milky Way arching over the desert mountains like a colossal bridge of light. A panorama of the Milky Way is a puzzle of both art and precision. You must shoot a sequence of overlapping frames, each a long exposure that sucks in photons imperceptible to the human eye, then stitch them seamlessly together later. The margin for error is pitiless; move the camera by too much, and the stars won’t align. Use the wrong exposure, and the structure of the galaxy dissolves into noise or blur.

So Auckland did what any modern adventurer stranded at the edge of expertise would do: he asked the AI. “I want to learn how to do panorama Milky Way photos,” he told Claude. “Give me some bullet points on how to achieve that.” The assistant responded with a condensed curriculum: the mechanics of exposure time, the trade-offs of aperture and ISO, the geometry of overlap between frames, the software for stitching, the necessity of finding a horizon free of human light. In a few exchanges, the abstract terror of the project turned into a checklist.

Empowered by this immediate, non-judgmental guidance, Auckland headed for Death Valley—a national park so vast and forbidding that, as he notes, “you can get lost pretty easily if you’re not smart about it.” He chose a spot where the Milky Way would arc right over a jagged ridgeline, providing the terrestrial anchor that gives astrophotography its scale. The plan was to rotate the camera about 30 degrees between shots, enough to overlap each frame and later merge them into a single, massive image of the mountain and the sky.

Then came the delicate dance of exposure. A typical starting point for Milky Way panoramas is what photographers call the “500 rule” or a variant thereof—a guideline to avoid star trails by limiting shutter speed based on your lens’s focal length. But real-world conditions refuse rigid formulas. Auckland settled on a 25-second exposure, let the shutter close, and checked the result on the camera’s screen. No. Not enough light. “We’re definitely not there yet. A little longer, I think,” he murmurs into the dark. When you are shivering beside a tripod in the desert at 2 a.m., the distance between a 25-second exposure and a 30-second exposure can feel like a chasm. Each adjustment risks dragging pinprick stars into tiny streaks, wasting the last precious hours of true night.

Yet this is precisely the process Claude couldn’t do for him—the real-time intuition born of staring at a histogram on a dim LCD and trusting your gut. The AI could outline the principles; the human had to stand in the cold and fail. “You’re gonna fail, but you got to keep going and keep pushing forward and just keep thinking,” Auckland says, and it’s not just bravado. It’s a description of how mastery works when the target is ephemeral.

What makes this collision of ancient landscape and cutting-edge tool so resonant is the friction between the visible and the invisible. The naked eye sees only an absence of light pollution and a placid darkness. The camera, with its aperture opened wide, acts as a time machine, accumulating seconds of deep-space photons onto a sensor until the Milky Way emerges in shocking color—the brown and cobalt knots of interstellar dust, the glow of billions of stars rendered as a single veil. “All of a sudden you’re seeing outer space,” Auckland says, the wonder still fresh in his voice.

It’s tempting to frame this story as a parable about technology supplanting human effort. But that misses the point. Claude didn’t photograph the stars. Auckland drove hours into the emptiness, scouted the location, set the tripod, iterated on his settings, and endured the peculiar silence of the middle of nowhere—a silence he describes as “such an awesome feeling.” The AI functioned like an infinitely patient field guide, collapsing a steep learning curve into actionable steps. It removed the intimidation, not the difficulty. The difficulty was the whole point.

As the night wore on and the Milky Way wheeled imperceptibly overhead, Auckland’s desperation mixed with his stubbornness. “I mean I hope we can get it tonight, that would be so sick. I’d be very excited.” The “we” is telling. He wasn’t solo; he carried a mentor in his pocket, one that never slept and never scolded him for asking the same question twice. For a person who likes things that are hard and that require a lot of practice, this might be the perfect partnership: an artificial mind to light the path, and a human will to walk it, into the dark, until the invisible finally reveals itself.
